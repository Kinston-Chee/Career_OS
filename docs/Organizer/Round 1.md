## 01 — Welcome

# From Ben

A note from Talentbank's CEO before you go any further.

Welcome, and thank you for putting your name forward.

I've spent fifteen years at Talentbank watching one pattern repeat itself. Bright graduates and the right opportunities miss each other in the dark — not because either side is doing something wrong, but because the systems sitting between them were never built to see the whole picture.

Job boards see jobs. ATS systems see filters. Universities see graduation. Nobody sees a career as one continuous thing.

Career OS is our attempt to change that.

Not to predict anyone's future — careers are too messy and too lucky for that — but to give people a better view of the landscape they're already in. A navigation tool that respects that the person using it gets to choose.

We don't think we have all the answers, and that's part of why this hackathon exists. We've shipped an internal prototype using AI tools, but it's clearly not the final shape of the thing. There are problems we haven't named, audiences we haven't served well, and angles we genuinely haven't seen.

That's where you come in.

The brief is freehand on purpose. We'd rather read a thoughtful submission that takes a real position than a polished one that hedges everything.

If anything is unclear, write to us. We read every email.

## The Honest Framing

# Career OS Is a Navigation Tool, Not a Prediction Tool

It doesn't tell anyone what their career **will** be.

It helps them see the paths available, the realistic ranges of outcomes for people like them, and the trade-offs each path implies.

The user keeps agency.

We just give them better data than they could ever assemble alone.

---

### What Career OS Should Help Users Do

- **See the landscape, not a single answer.**  
  Surface the realistic range of trajectories for people with a similar shape — skills, education, prior roles, geography — and explain the trade-offs of each.

- **Understand career as continuous, not transactional.**  
  A career isn't a sequence of job applications. It's a 40-year arc with phases, plateaus, pivots, and compounding skills. The system should hold that long view.

- **Speak human language back to humans.**  
  Explain why a recommendation makes sense and where the uncertainty sits. No black-box scores, no false precision.

- **Connect both sides honestly.**  
  Candidates need to be findable at the right moment for the right reason. Employers need to spot the right person before they're publicly looking — without the spam and noise that makes both sides cynical.
